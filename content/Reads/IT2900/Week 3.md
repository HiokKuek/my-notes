###  Fast Company: [How to spot the warning signs of an insecure leader (and how to work with one)](https://www.fastcompany.com/90656628/how-to-spot-the-warning-signs-of-an-insecure-leader-and-how-to-work-with-one)
- **Confidence ≠ Competence**
    - People often mistake arrogance and certainty for leadership ability. Overconfident leaders may be insecure and lack true skills.
- **Humility is a Strength**
    - Effective leaders admit what they don’t know, are self-aware, and leverage the strengths of others. Humility correlates with better team performance.
- **Arrogance Often Masks Insecurity**
    - Narcissistic or self-aggrandizing behavior can be a cover for self-doubt and a need for validation.
- **Modern Leadership Requires Psychological Capital**
    - Beyond knowledge or social networks, today’s leaders need empathy, curiosity, creativity, and emotional intelligence.
- **Arrogant Leaders Reduce Psychological Safety**
    - Teams under insecure or arrogant leaders are less likely to speak up, increasing risk of failure.
- **Selecting Leaders**
    - Look past style (charisma, social network, achievements) and prioritize actual competence, humility, and the ability to develop others.
- **Working with Insecure Leaders**
    - Avoid direct confrontation; instead, support the team, provide constructive feedback through proper channels (e.g., 360 reviews), and protect team performance.

### [Gallup: The Impact of Positive Leadership - How seemingly small interactions can dramatically boost your team's productivity](https://news.gallup.com/businessjournal/11458/impact-positive-leadership.aspx) 
- **Positive-to-Negative Ratio (PNR) Matters**
    - Small positive interactions at work (like greetings, compliments, or small acts of help) have a big impact on employees’ moods and productivity.
    - Negative interactions (criticisms, jabs, complaints) can quickly outweigh positives.
- **The “Magic Ratio”**
    - Research (Gottman, Fredrickson, Losada) shows a **minimum PNR of 3:1** in workplaces improves performance and engagement.
    - Extremely high ratios (e.g., 13:1) can be counterproductive; positive emotions need to be grounded in reality.
- **Examples of Positive Leadership in Action**
    - Suzie’s story: small positive interactions (smile from receptionist, elevator held) vs. negative ones (boss’s jab for being late) show how leaders influence employee engagement.
    - Ken, a CEO, actively spreads positive emotions by acknowledging achievements and personal milestones across offices.
- **Impact of Positive Leadership**
    - Positive leaders improve:
        - Team mood
        - Job satisfaction
        - Engagement
        - Overall performance
- **How Positive Leaders Operate**
    - They actively look for opportunities to celebrate and recognize others.
    - They invest in employees’ well-being, not just outcomes.
    - They create a ripple effect where positive energy spreads throughout the organization.
